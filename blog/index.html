Who is leading the space race?

Author: Oscar Uwayo

The space race was initially between two cold war rivals, the Soviet Union (USSR) and the United States of America. It was on Friday Oct 4, 1957 when the world marked the genesis of space exploration with the successful launch of Sputnik-1 of the former Soviet Union.  Unexpectedly, three months later, the US Navy launched Vanguard TV-3, but it did not go as expected. After the booster ignited, in about 2 seconds, Vanguard TV-3 lost thrust, and the fuel tank exploded. Regardless of the upps and down, many countries have been launching rockets so often, and it made me wonder who is leading the space race?

 Using the Kaggle data of all launches from 1957-2020, I counted the number of rockets launched by each country. In Figure 1, Russia seems to dominate in space, and the United States of America follows. The results were surprising to me because I barely hear about Russia; most of the time, US media announces rocket launches and various space missions. But then, Russia’s media is not popular, and many people do not follow what is going on inside the country.

                     Figure 1: Top 7 countries and number of launches. 

Looking at successful missions’ various countries have accomplished over the years, just after the Vanguard Tv-3 failure, it is promising a sustainable human presence somewhere beyond the earth. Taking the United States of America that was able to send a man to the moon and back for the first time in 1969, and many space missions that were tried after, it shows dedication and continuous learning that has kept humanity going. Regardless of many obstacles faced like launch failure, but overall, Figure 2 shows that we are getting better at launching rockets. By analysing the continuous increase of successful missions, possibly that could inform the odds of human beings colonizing other planets. In Figure 2, it seems that from around 1960 to 2020 the successful launches have been increasing from 21% to about 97%, and that is tremendous improvement.

            Figure 2: Distribution of success missions’ over the years 

As we appreciate the progress in space exploration, I also wonder whether the dream of making life multiplanetary will be a reality. In Figure 3, by grouping the total expenditure each year, I could see general decline since 1970, and a spike in the late 1980s. But then, it seemed that from somewhere in 1990 the expenditure had been dropping down till today. In other words, we can say advancement in technology is reducing the cost of launching rockets, and of course, people in this industry are progressively learning which is another imperative to this outstanding decline. 

               Figure 3: Total expenditure on the rockets launched over the years

What might be more surprising as mentioned above is how somewhere in 1988, the total expenditure increased that high; it was the launch of a fully automated and reusable space shuttle named Energia-Buran by the Soviet Union that cost around $1.7 billion, and that is the notable key contributed to such unusual spike.  As launch gets more affordable, possibly in some future that is not beyond our reach, space exploration will be commercially open to individuals and the beginning of life on other planets.
